## 10. Gap Analysis: Why Existing Literature Is Insufficient (Paper Positioning)

### Gap 1: Network-level vs. Link-level
Most studies estimate average rainfall-speed effects at network or city level (e.g., Wang et al. 2022; Elhenawy et al. 2021). Very few decompose sensitivity to the individual road segment. Your paper estimates 460 link-specific precipitation coefficients — unprecedented granularity for the UK SRN.

### Gap 2: Simulation-heavy vs. Rapid empirical model ⭐ (Core Gap)
Existing transport-climate resilience tools overwhelmingly rely on ABM/microsimulation (He et al. 2025; Huang et al. 2022) or coupled flood-traffic models (Pregnolato et al. 2017, 2024) that require: HPC/cloud computing, city-specific calibration, proprietary OD matrices, flood inundation modelling (CityCAT etc.), and months of setup. The UNECE (2024) stress test framework recognises the need for rapid quantitative screening tools, but few exist. The DfT REA (2024) explicitly notes empirical weather-disruption models exist for rail but are lacking for roads. **Your Gamma GLM is a rapid model**: open data (WebTRIS + Met Office) → runs on a laptop → produces link-level coefficients in hours → no OD matrices, no flood model, no HPC.

### Gap 3: Describing sensitivity but not explaining it
Studies like Lu et al. (2024) cluster roads by resilience pattern but don't use regression to identify which road features drive the heterogeneity. Your Stage 2 OLS directly regresses precipitation coefficients on lanes, speed_limit, daily_flow, length, rural — providing causal interpretation for planners.

### Gap 4: UK SRN lacks empirical climate sensitivity evidence
National Highways reports (ORR 2024, NCE 2026) show only 7% of SRN meets climate standards and 36% has significant flood susceptibility, but there is no published empirical analysis quantifying how each SRN segment's operating speed responds to rainfall. Your paper fills this directly.

### Gap 5: No open-source, replicable framework
Existing tools (VAST, OpenCLIM, IMP) are either US-focused, still under development, or require GIS expertise. Your Streamlit platform + GitHub release provides a ready-to-use, transferable tool for any UK local authority.
