# Writing Style Guide for TRD/JTG
## Based on analysis of Gao (2024), Pregnolato (2017), Pulugurtha (2021), Becker (2026), Li (2024)

---

## 1. Structure & Length

**TRD typical sections:**
1. Introduction (1.5–2 pages)
2. Literature Review (2–3 pages, separate numbered section)
3. Methodology (3–5 pages)
4. Study Area and Data / Results and Discussion (4–6 pages)
5. Conclusions (1–1.5 pages)

**Total:** 7,000–10,000 words body text. 15–20 pages (Elsevier single-column).

**Key:** Both TRD and JTG strongly prefer a **dedicated, separate Literature Review section** (Section 2).

---

## 2. Introduction Pattern (5–7 paragraphs)

1. **Context** — broad importance (3–6 citations)
2. **Problem narrowing** — specific phenomenon
3. **Gap identification** — "However, limited research has focused on..."
4. **Research aims** — explicit questions or aims
5. **Methodology preview** — brief sketch
6. **Contributions** — bulleted/numbered list
7. **Paper organization** — "The rest of this paper is organized as follows..."

**Gap phrases:**
- "However, limited research has focused on..."
- "To our best knowledge, no researchers have yet..."
- "existing approaches fail to capture..."

**Contribution phrases:**
- "This paper makes several contributions, outlined as follows."
- "the overall contribution of this research is four-fold: 1)... 2)..."

---

## 3. Literature Review Style

- **Thematic**, not chronological
- Clearly labeled subsections (e.g., 2.1 Weather impacts, 2.2 Statistical approaches)
- **End each subsection with a "However..." gap statement**
- Citation density: 5–8 per paragraph
- Weaving: "Author et al. (Year) found..." + "(Author1; Author2; Author3)" clusters

---

## 4. Methodology Presentation

- Every equation numbered, every variable defined immediately after
- Pattern: verbal description → equation → "where X is/denotes..."
- Model justification: cite prior work, compare candidates, explain rationale
- **Include a methodological framework diagram (Fig. 1)**
- Break into numbered steps

---

## 5. Results Presentation

- Tables: clean minimal formatting, descriptive titles, coefficients with SE and p-values
- Figures referenced before they appear: "as shown in Fig. 4"
- Statistical metrics: R², p-values as "<0.01", RMSE, AIC/BIC
- Narrate sequentially: "It can be observed that...", "Results show that..."

---

## 6. Discussion Style

- Restate finding → explain mechanism → connect to prior literature → practical implications
- "consistent with findings by Author (Year)"
- "This may be attributed to..."
- **Limitations always explicitly discussed**
- **Future work always included**

---

## 7. Key Academic Phrases

| Category | Phrases |
|----------|---------|
| Gap | "However, limited research...", "To our best knowledge...", "have not received equal attention" |
| Contribution | "This research provides further evidence on...", "this study offers a necessary contribution" |
| Results | "Results reveal/indicate/suggest that...", "It can be observed that..." |
| Comparison | "consistent with prior literature...", "This may be attributed to..." |
| Hedging | "may", "suggests", "is likely to", "could be due to" |

---

## 8. Transition Words (high frequency)

However, Therefore, Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally, Notably, Nevertheless, Consequently, Subsequently, Specifically, In contrast, Meanwhile

---

## 9. Citation Density

| Section | Citations per paragraph |
|---------|----------------------|
| Introduction | 4–7 |
| Literature Review | 5–8 |
| Methodology | 1–3 |
| Results | 0–2 |
| Discussion | 2–4 |
| Conclusion | 0–1 |
| **Total references** | **45–70** |

---

## 10. Voice & Tense

- **Active voice** accepted: "We develop...", "We found that..."
- **Present tense** for contributions: "This study introduces..."
- **Past tense** for methods: "Data were collected..."
- **Present perfect** for lit review: "Previous research has suggested..."
- Use "this study/research" as subject freely
